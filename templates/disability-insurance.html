What is Disability Insurance?

Disability insurance is a type of health insurance that replaces a portion of your income if you become unable to work due to an illness or injury. Disability insurance can help you cover your essential living expenses and protect your financial security.

There are two main types of disability insurance: short-term and long-term. Short-term disability insurance provides benefits for a few weeks or months, usually up to one year. Long-term disability insurance provides benefits for several years or until you reach retirement age, depending on the policy.

The amount of benefits you receive from disability insurance depends on your income, the percentage of income replacement, and the benefit cap. For example, if you earn $5,000 a month, your policy replaces 60% of your income, and the cap is $3,000, you will receive $3,000 a month in benefits.

The cost of disability insurance depends on several factors, such as your age, health, occupation, income, benefit amount, benefit period, elimination period, and policy features. The elimination period is the waiting time between the start of your disability and the start of your benefits. The longer the elimination period, the lower the premium.

You can get disability insurance through your employer, a professional association, or an individual policy. Employer-sponsored disability insurance is usually cheaper and easier to qualify for, but it may not cover all your needs, and it may not be portable if you change jobs. Individual disability insurance is more expensive and requires medical underwriting, but it offers more customization and portability.
